# Detection Tools Reference

Detection tools are probabilistic signals, not proof. Use them to find text that may need better sourcing, clearer authorship, or more natural academic revision. Do not use them as a guarantee that text is original or human-written.

## Turnitin / iThenticate

Primary focus: similarity against published, web, and institutional sources.

Common flags:
- Long exact phrase matches.
- Near-identical sentence order across a paragraph.
- Patchwriting: small synonym swaps over the same source structure.
- Missing quotation marks around copied language.
- Bibliography and boilerplate if filters are not enabled.

Good response:
- Add citations where ideas come from sources.
- Quote short exact language when necessary.
- Rewrite from notes, not beside the source sentence.
- Change argument structure, not only vocabulary.

## GPTZero

Primary focus: statistical signals often described as perplexity and burstiness.

Common flags:
- Uniform sentence length.
- Generic transitions.
- Repetitive paragraph structure.
- Low specificity.
- Overly polished, balanced prose.

Good response:
- Add concrete evidence and domain-specific detail.
- Vary paragraph and sentence shape naturally.
- Keep author voice and uncertainty where appropriate.

## Originality.ai

Primary focus: AI-likelihood classification plus plagiarism checks depending on product mode.

Common flags:
- Predictable phrasing.
- Formulaic introductions and conclusions.
- Repeated summary patterns.
- Thin claims without context or citations.

Good response:
- Preserve factual accuracy.
- Add source-grounded nuance.
- Use citations and discipline-specific terminology.
- Avoid claims that exceed the evidence.

## Practical Rule

If a detector flags text, inspect it manually. Ask:

1. Is source attribution complete?
2. Are any phrases copied exactly?
3. Does the paragraph follow the source too closely?
4. Are claims specific and verifiable?
5. Does the voice match the rest of the paper?
